Stop Punishing Your Users: The Case for Just-in-Time Security Training

Most organizations respond to phishing simulation failures the same way: assign a video, check a box, move on. It feels like accountability, but it's really just ''Secuirty Theater''. Just-in-time training works differently, and the gap in outcomes is worth understanding.

The assumption built into most awareness programs

When someone clicks a simulated phishing link, the standard response is to assign them remedial training. This usually takes the form of a video with a short quiz attached. The logic is sound: the user made a mistake so we educate them. However, a user often sees it as "My Security department made me look like a fool, now I'm being punished by being forced to watch this video."

Your users already know phishing is dangerous. They've sat through the annual awareness training. They've seen the posters. The failure isn't ignorance, it's a momentary lapse in judgment under realistic conditions. Sending them a twelve-minute video three days later doesn't address that.

What forced video training actually accomplishes

What really happens when an employee fails a phishing simulation and gets assigned remedial training? They feel embarrassed, probably even resentful. They complete the video because they have to, not because they want to, which is not indicative of true learning. This resentment can fracture already difficult relationships with users as a whole, potentially driving down phish reports and overall buy-in to the organization's Cybersecurity program.

Security awareness programs live and die by reporting rates. If your users are quietly learning that engaging with security leads to punishment, or is a hassle in general, you are undermining the one behavior that matters most when a real attack lands in an inbox. A user who clicks a real phishing link and says nothing because they fear consequences is a far greater risk than one who clicks and immediately reports it.

Just-in-time training operates on a different principle

Just-in-time (JIT) training occurs at the moment of failure rather than hours or days afterward. When a user clicks a simulated phishing link, instead of a landing page followed by a delayed training assignment, they are immediately shown a brief, targeted explanation of what just happened and why it was a threat indicator. Many JIT solutions also offer a brief quiz based off of the test email itself, forcing the user to identify the missed red flags. The learning occurs in context, at the exact moment the user is most receptive to it.

Immediate feedback is consistently more effective than delayed feedback at producing behavior change. A user who has just clicked a simulated phishing link is engaged, attentive, and primed to receive information in a way that a user completing a training assigned three days later is not.

JIT training can be designed to feel instructive rather than punitive. "Here is what you just encountered and here is how to spot it next time" lands very differently than "you failed; complete the following training." One builds competence while the other builds avoidance.

What the outcomes look like in practice

Organizations that shift from punitive remediation to JIT intervention typically see improvement across the two metrics that matter most: a decline in click rates on subsequent simulations, and phishing reporting rates increase. These outcomes tend to move together, which makes sense. When users stop associating the simulation program with negative consequences, they engage with it differently. They become participants rather than subjects.

In my own programs, the transition to a JIT model was one of several factors that contributed to meaningful improvement in reporting rates over a twelve-month period. Separating the precise contribution of JIT training from other program changes is difficult in a real operational environment, but the trend is consistent: decrease in failure rates and an increase in reporting rates.

The objection security leaders raise

The most common pushback I hear is about accountability. "If we don't assign training after a failure, aren't we just letting people off the hook?" It's a reasonable question, especially in regulated environments where documented training completion is often required by cyber insurance policies and other regulatory requirments.

There are two responses worth making here. First, JIT training is training. It can be logged, tracked, and counted toward compliance requirements just as traditional assignments can. Second, the goal of a security awareness program is not to document that training occurred. It is to reduce the likelihood of a successful attack. Those are related but not identical objectives. When compliance requirements drive program design in ways that undermine behavioral outcomes, the compliance is being served at the expense of security. That trade-off deserves more scrutiny than it typically receives. Again, Security Theater.

A more useful framing for the CISO conversation

If you are a security leader evaluating your awareness program, the question worth asking is not "are our users completing their training?" It is "are our users more likely to report a suspicious email today than they were twelve months ago?" Click rates on simulations are a useful signal but an incomplete one. Reporting rates tell you something more important: whether your users have internalized security as a shared responsibility rather than an external compliance burden.

JIT training, when implemented thoughtfully, is one of the more reliable methods for moving that metric. It's not a silver bullet, but it presses the advantage when a user is most receptive to a learning opportunity and removes the punative nature historically seen in phish awareness training.

Phishing simulations are only as valuable as the behavioral change they drive. If your program is generating completion records but flat reporting rates, the design deserves a second look. The technology to deliver JIT training exists in most major awareness platforms. The harder work is convincing stakeholders that accountability and punishment are not the same thing.
